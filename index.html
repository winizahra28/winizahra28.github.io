Pulp, Paper & Forestry

Driving Digital Adoption at Scale: SAP S/4 HANA Transformation

Leading Global Pulp & Paper Manufacturer — in partnership with Accenture
1,000+
End Users
30%
Efficiency Gain
6

The Challenge

A Fortune Global 500 pulp and paper manufacturer was implementing SAP S/4 HANA across its entire value chain — from forestry operations (nursery, plantation, harvesting) to manufacturing (Pindo Deli, OKI Mills). The transformation impacted 1,000+ end users across 6 regions, with significant resistance risks due to deeply entrenched legacy processes and varying digital literacy levels across operational sites.

My Approach
  • Conducted comprehensive Change Impact Assessments and Organization Impact Assessments for every business process, mapping changes, risks, and benefits at the process level
  • Designed competency-based training programs targeting specific skill gaps across 4 new business processes, building training curriculums from scratch with needs analysis and post-training assessments
  • Calculated Full-Time Equivalent (FTE) for 1,400+ end users to provide data-driven workforce optimization recommendations post-implementation
  • Built and executed multi-channel communication programs across all operational regions, using infographics, stakeholder briefings, and on-site engagement sessions
  • Managed change readiness tracking to proactively identify and address resistance hotspots before each deployment wave
The Results
  • Achieved approximately 30% efficiency improvement in operational performance post-SAP deployment
  • Reached 80% training completion rate and 80% skill improvement across all target user groups
  • Achieved approximately 70% positive adoption feedback from end users
  • Successfully deployed across Pindo Deli, OKI Mills, and Forestry divisions on schedule
Key Lesson

In large-scale ERP transformations with diverse user populations, one-size-fits-all training fails. The breakthrough came from segmenting users by digital literacy level and operational context, then tailoring training intensity and format accordingly. Field operators needed hands-on simulation; managers needed process-flow workshops. This segmentation was the single biggest driver of the 80% skill improvement outcome.

Engineering & Construction

Cross-Client Change Management in Engineering & Construction

Leading Indonesian EPC Company — 5 Multinational Clients
5
Clients
4
Business Systems
The Challenge

A major Indonesian engineering, procurement, and construction (EPC) firm was simultaneously rolling out 4 new enterprise systems — Manpower Planning, Document Management, Management System, and Project Productivity Management — while serving active projects for 5 multinational clients: Pertamina, POSCO, Shell, Pupuk Kaltim, and LCo2. The challenge was managing change at the corporate level while adapting to client-specific project requirements.

My Approach
  • Led full-cycle change management from scoping through adoption, covering corporate-level systems and project-level operational workflows
  • Conducted change impact assessments across 3 core disciplines: engineering, procurement, and construction — each with distinct process cultures
  • Designed scalable change frameworks that could flex between corporate standardization and client-specific project demands
  • Managed training needs analysis and skill development programs for managers and field teams simultaneously
The Results
  • Successfully deployed all 4 systems from corporate to project level within the planned timeline
  • Optimized site operational management workflows across engineering, procurement, and construction
  • Created a reusable change management framework adaptable to future EPC client engagements
Key Lesson

When managing change across multiple simultaneous client engagements in EPC, the critical success factor is separating "core change" (corporate systems and processes that are non-negotiable) from "adaptive change" (client-specific workflows that need flexibility). Trying to standardize everything creates resistance; allowing everything to be custom creates chaos. The framework needs a clear core with defined flex points.

State-Owned Enterprises

Unifying Three SOEs Under a Single Transformation Agenda

Survey & Inspection Holding — Cross-Entity Integration
3
Sub-Holdings
The Challenge

A newly formed state-owned holding company needed to integrate three established survey and inspection entities — each with distinct corporate cultures, governance structures, and operational systems — under a unified transformation agenda. The challenge extended beyond systems integration to cultural alignment and governance harmonization.

My Approach
  • Designed a cross-entity Change Management Program that respected the identity of each subsidiary while building toward unified operating principles
  • Conducted comprehensive Change Impact Assessments across systems, roles, governance, and culture at all three sub-holdings
  • Identified and mitigated high-impact transformation risks unique to each entity, creating entity-specific mitigation plans within a shared framework
  • Developed change communication strategies that balanced holding-level messaging with subsidiary-level relevance
Key Lesson

Merging cultures is harder than merging systems. When integrating multiple organizations, the biggest mistake is leading with systems and process standardization. Leading with a shared vision and governance clarity — then layering systems on top — creates the psychological safety needed for people to let go of "how we've always done it."

Government / Public Sector

Modernizing IT Governance Across 100+ Diplomatic Missions

Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the Republic of Indonesia
100+
Embassies
The Challenge

Indonesia's Ministry of Foreign Affairs faced significant challenges in IT governance across its global network of 100+ embassies. Decentralized IT management, varying levels of local technical capability, and the unique challenge of regular ambassadorial rotations created a fragmented technology landscape that hindered modernization efforts.

My Approach
  • Delivered organizational and human resource analysis for global IT divisions, mapping decentralized governance challenges across embassy networks worldwide
  • Facilitated workload and job role change assessments to recommend structure modernization and capability alignment
  • Drafted change communications and upskilling strategy specifically tied to ambassadorial rotation cycles and local IT readiness levels
  • Developed recommendations for sustainable capability building that accounts for frequent leadership transitions
Key Lesson

Government transformation requires a fundamentally different change management cadence. Unlike corporate transformations with clear executive mandates, diplomatic institutions operate on rotation cycles and consensus. Change strategies must be designed to survive leadership transitions — embedding adoption into institutional processes rather than relying on individual champions who will inevitably rotate out.
